# PBefG-Genehmigungsmanager

**Pillar:** [[shared-clean-mobility]]
**One-liner:** Multi-Bundesland licensing automation platform for ride-pooling and demand-responsive transport operators navigating the PBefG 2021 Gebündelte Bedarfsverkehre framework.
**Shortlist score:** 24/35
**Tags:** #b2b #saas #compliance #mobility

## Problem

The Personenbeförderungsgesetz reform of August 2021 created a legal category for demand-responsive pooled transport (Gebündelte Bedarfsverkehre) — covering ride-pooling, on-demand shuttles, and hybrid transit. However, operators must obtain a separate Genehmigung (licence) from each Genehmigungsbehörde (transport authority) in each operating area — 16 Bundesländer, each with different forms, timelines (4–6 months), and reporting requirements. ViaVan, MOIA, door2door, and smaller operators have all described the licensing process as their primary growth bottleneck, requiring a dedicated regulatory affairs team. A single operator expanding to 5 cities needs 5 separate licensing processes, each with its own Antrag format, Pflichtversicherungsnachweis, and operating-concept documentation.

## Regulatory driver / Why now

- PBefG §50 (Gebündelte Bedarfsverkehre) in force since August 2021; licensing uptake growing.
- Federal government's Nationaler Mobilitätsplan 2030 calls for accelerating demand-responsive transport licensing.
- Municipalities increasingly inviting tenders for on-demand services to replace thin bus routes.

## Target users

- **End users:** regulatory affairs teams at ride-pooling operators; municipal transit authorities tendering on-demand services.
- **Payers:**
  - Mobility startups (door2door, MOIA, Moia): €1,000–3,000/month per active Bundesland.
  - Municipal transit authorities: €500–1,500/month for the reverse (issuing side).

## Solution

A regulatory workflow platform that:
1. Maintains a living database of PBefG Genehmigung requirements per Bundesland and per operating city.
2. Guides operators through each Antrag with pre-filled templates and checklist completion tracking.
3. Manages the Pflichtversicherungsnachweis, vehicle-type documentation, and operating-concept uploads.
4. Tracks licence status, renewal dates, and reporting obligations per operating area.
5. Notifies operators of regulatory changes (new Bundesland ordinances, court rulings on PBefG scope).

## Business model

- **SaaS subscription** per Bundesland operating area.
- **Bundesland database licence** for law firms and transport consultancies.
- **Municipal edition:** reverse workflow for authorities managing incoming applications.

## Market size (top-down)

- ~80 active ride-pooling and on-demand operators in Germany; ~300 expected by 2027.
- At €18k average ACV per operator (3 Bundesländer average) and 15% penetration: SAM €3.6m. Broader EU expansion (PBefG analogues in Austria, Netherlands) adds TAM.

## Competitive landscape

- **Regulatory affairs consultancies (PricewaterhouseCoopers Legal, Taylor Wessing):** advisory only.
- **LexisNexis, Nomos:** legal databases without workflow automation.
- No dedicated PBefG licensing workflow product exists.

## Moat and differentiation

- Bundesland-by-Bundesland database is manually maintained and not easily replicated.
- Historical licence applications create a template library that accelerates new-city launches.
- Municipal edition creates a two-sided network: operators and authorities both on-platform.

## Regulatory path (RDG)

Moderate RDG risk: helping operators complete Genehmigungsanträge could be construed as legal services. Mitigation: present as a document-preparation tool with templates; do not provide legal interpretation; partner with a transport-law firm for legal sign-off on operating concepts.

## Key risks

- Bundesländer standardise the PBefG licensing form, reducing the complexity advantage.
- Market remains too small if ride-pooling adoption stalls.
- Large operators hire in-house regulatory affairs teams.
