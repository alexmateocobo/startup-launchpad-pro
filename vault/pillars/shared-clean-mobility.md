# Shared Clean Mobility

**Track:** [[mobility]]

## Description

Regulatory operations tooling for operators of shared micro-mobility (e-scooters, e-mopeds, cargo bikes) and demand-responsive transport (ride-pooling, on-demand shuttles) navigating a fragmented, city-by-city permit landscape. Two regulations define the market: the Elektrokleinstfahrzeuge-Verordnung (eKFV, 2019) for micro-mobility, and the PBefG §50 reform (August 2021) for demand-responsive pooled transport. Both create licensing obligations that must be fulfilled separately in each city or Bundesland — with different forms, timelines, and documentation requirements per authority. Operators managing 20 German cities employ 3–5 FTE on compliance alone. Cities are increasingly moving to competitive tendering (Ausschreibung) with performance-based KPIs, raising the compliance bar further.

## Key Regulations

| Regulation | What it requires | In force |
|---|---|---|
| eKFV (Elektrokleinstfahrzeuge-Verordnung) | Legal framework for e-scooter operations; cities add ordinances for zones, parking, fleet caps | Since June 2019 |
| PBefG §50 (Gebündelte Bedarfsverkehre) | Legal category for ride-pooling and on-demand transport; separate Genehmigung per Bundesland | Since August 2021 |
| EU Urban Mobility Framework | Municipalities must measure and report on sustainable mobility and mode shift | Since 2021 |
| Nationaler Radverkehrsplan 3.0 | Drives cities toward performance-based permits tied to safety and parking data | Ongoing |
| MDS (Mobility Data Specification) | Data-sharing standard increasingly required by cities for micro-mobility permits | City-by-city |

## Market Size

- **eKFV micro-mobility:** ~8 major operators active in Germany across 50+ cities. At €24k average ACV per operator (10-city average) and 50% penetration: SAM €10m Germany; TAM €40m EU.
- **PBefG ride-pooling:** ~80 active operators in Germany today; ~300 expected by 2027. At €18k average ACV (3 Bundesländer average) and 15% penetration: SAM €3.6m; growing with EU analogues.

## Best-in-Class Benchmarks

- **Populus** — benchmark for MDS data pipeline between operators and city mobility authorities; demonstrates what two-sided network effects look like in this space.
- **Hamburg model** — Hamburg's mandatory parking-station requirement and competitive tender for micro-mobility (2024) is the leading example of city-level performance-based permitting.
- **MOIA (Hamburg)** — the most advanced ride-pooling deployment in Germany; their regulatory operations define what a scaled PBefG operator requires.
